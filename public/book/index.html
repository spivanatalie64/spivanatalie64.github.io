Chapter 1

Origins: Oklahoma, OSU, and Finding My Path

My name is Natalie Cole-Clift Spiva, and I was born on September 15, 2001. I grew up in a world that kept telling me I was supposed to be someone named Johnathan, but I knew from the very beginning that wasn't the whole story. Oklahoma is where I started, and it shaped me in ways I am still discovering. Oklahoma isn't the kind of place people expect to produce a trans girl who builds operating systems, but that's exactly what it did. The DIY attitude, the skepticism of authority, the refusal to be told what to do — all of that is pure Oklahoma, and I carry it with me every single day.

I enrolled at Oklahoma State University with the same hopes everyone carries into college: that this would be my path, my future, my launchpad. It lasted about a semester before I knew, with absolute certainty, that the system was not built for someone like me. Too much bureaucracy, too much busywork, not enough actual building. I didn't drop out so much as I dropped into something else — I dropped into building my own path. When people ask me about OSU now, I tell them the truth: it wasn't for me, and I knew it almost immediately. That decision wasn't failure; it was clarity. It was the first time I looked at a system that wasn't working for me and said "no" — a pattern that would define the rest of my life.

I am the co-creator and co-lead developer of AcreetionOS, an Arch Linux-based operating system distribution built from the ground up to be user-friendly while maintaining the power and flexibility that Arch users demand. The project started as a question: what if Arch was truly accessible? What if a distro could be libre, fast, and simple without sacrificing the control that makes Arch special? Those questions became an obsession, and that obsession became an operating system. AcreetionOS now ships with the Cinnamon desktop environment, XLibre display server support, PipeWire audio, and a curated selection of over 250 packages — all built, tested, and verified by a tiny team of two people who refuse to compromise on quality.

I am twenty-four years old and I currently live in Spokane, Washington, specifically in the Bemiss neighborhood. Before that, I was in Oklahoma, and the transition from the southern plains to the Pacific Northwest was about more than geography — it was about finding a place where I could be fully, completely myself. Spokane isn't perfect, but it's home now. I film video content around my neighborhood, I know my streets, and I've built my life here brick by brick. Washington State also gives me legal protections that Oklahoma never did, and as a trans woman in 2026, those protections matter more than I can express.

This fall, I am starting classes at Spokane Community College. It's a new chapter, and I'm approaching it with the same intensity I bring to everything else. I already have a vocational degree as a Network and PC Support Specialist, and I recently earned a Gold level certification on the Oklahoma WorkKeys job-skills assessment — an achievement I'm genuinely proud of. But SCC represents something bigger: a commitment to never stop learning, never stop growing, and never stop proving that I can do things my own way. I'm twenty-four, I've already built an operating system, and I'm just getting started.

I am also the founder or co-founder of a dizzying number of side projects that have spun out of my need to fix everything that's broken in the software world. YAcFS (Yet Another common File System) was born from my frustration with the licensing hell of ZFS and the data-corruption nightmares of btrfs. FreedomOffice is my fork of LibreOffice with a Microsoft Office-compatible ribbon UI and zero telemetry. SprunglesQT is the ultra-lightweight Qt desktop I built by stripping LXQt of its bloat. Veskto and Datcord are Gecko-powered Discord clients for people who value their RAM as much as their privacy. FlatFree is a community Flatpak repository free and open for everyone. The list goes on, and every single project starts the same way: I see something broken, I get angry about it, and I build a replacement.

I am a transgender woman, and I have been actively transitioning since February of 2025. My legal name change from Johnathan Spiva to Natalie Cole-Clift Spiva is a matter of public court record. I am also, controversially to some, a Republican — not because I agree with every Republican politician or policy, but because I believe in individual liberty, personal responsibility, fiscal discipline, and the separation of identity from politics. I am living proof that a trans girl can hold conservative values without contradiction. Identity, religion, politics, and sexuality are separate things, and anyone who tells you otherwise is selling you a simplification that doesn't hold up to real life. My politics are my own, but my focus is on building great software for everyone, regardless of background.

Meridian Tech: The 4.0 That Proved Them Wrong

After OSU, I needed to find a place that actually taught me something useful. Meridian Technology Center was that place. While OSU buried me in theory and bureaucracy, Meridian Tech put tools in my hands and told me to build. The difference was night and day. At OSU I felt like a number being processed through a system; at Meridian I felt like a person learning a craft. The instructors respected that I wanted to do real work, not just pass exams. They understood that some people learn by building, not by listening to lectures, and they gave me the space to be the kind of student I actually am.

I earned a 4.0 GPA at Meridian Tech, and I want to be very clear about what that means: it wasn't about grades, and it wasn't about being the smartest person in the room. It was about finally finding work that mattered to me — work that I could pour myself into completely without having to pretend I was someone I wasn't. When you find the thing you're meant to do, excellence isn't a struggle; it's the natural outcome of genuine engagement. That 4.0 wasn't luck, and it wasn't natural talent. It was the result of finally being in an environment that respected my intelligence instead of trying to fit me into a mold I was never going to fit.

My vocational degree is in Network and PC Support, and every single skill I learned there has been directly applicable to everything I've built since. Understanding how networks actually work at the packet level, how hardware actually communicates, how systems actually boot — these aren't abstract concepts for me. They're the foundation of everything from AcreetionOS to my self-hosted infrastructure to my Android kernel work. Meridian Tech didn't just give me a degree; it gave me a mental model for how computers actually function, and I've been building on that model ever since.

The Gold level Oklahoma WorkKeys certification was another validation that I was on the right path. WorkKeys isn't a typical academic test; it measures applied skills — reading for information, locating information, applied mathematics. These are the skills that actually matter in the real world, and scoring at the Gold level meant that my years of hands-on tinkering had paid off in measurable, verifiable ways. I keep that certification as a reminder that practical knowledge beats theoretical knowledge every single time.

Meridian Tech also taught me something about education itself: it doesn't have to be broken. The system can work when it's designed around students instead of administrators. It can be respectful, practical, and genuinely useful. That experience gave me a standard to measure everything else against, and it's why I'm so excited about starting at Spokane Community College this fall. I know what good education looks like now, and I won't settle for anything less.

If you mess with Bre, you mess with me, and you do not want to mess with me. I embrace my own intensity when it comes to protecting the people I love. That intensity isn't a flaw; it's a feature. It's what keeps my people safe, and keeping my people safe is one of my core operating parameters. Bre is in Texas, but she is never alone, because she has me, and I am relentless.

Growing up in Oklahoma as someone assigned male at birth, the expectations were clear: be this, act like this, want these things. None of it fit. Harder than any technical challenge I have ever faced. My legal name change from Johnathan Spiva to Natalie Cole-Clift Spiva is a matter of public court record. That's a phrase I have to write a lot, because it carries legal weight. The name change order is a document that permanently connects my old name to my new name, and that link is both a legal necessity and a vulnerability. Anyone with access to court records can trace my transition, and in the current political climate of 2026, that exposure is not abstract — it is a concrete safety risk. I monitor for my deadname across the internet using a purpose-built surveillance system that scans dozens of data sources. This isn't paranoia; this is prudence.

Transition is still the hardest thing I do, every single day. It's not a one-time event; it's an ongoing process of becoming, of maintaining, of navigating a world that is not always kind to people like me. But I did it. I am doing it. Every shot of estrogen, every voice training session, every legal document updated, every moment of choosing to be authentic even when authenticity costs something — all of it is worth it. Being authentically myself makes every single hurdle worth it. I am Natalie, and that name is not just a label; it is the culmination of everything I have fought to become.

The Body I Live In: Ankylosing Spondylitis, RA, and Chronic Pain

I live with severe chronic pain caused by Ankylosing Spondylitis and Rheumatoid Arthritis. These are not minor conditions. Ankylosing Spondylitis is a type of inflammatory arthritis that primarily affects the spine and sacroiliac joints, causing vertebrae to fuse together over time in a process that is both painful and irreversible. Rheumatoid Arthritis is an autoimmune disorder that attacks the joints, causing inflammation, stiffness, and degeneration. Together, they create a daily reality where my mobility is heavily impacted, where pain is a constant companion rather than an occasional visitor, and where simply existing in my body requires more energy than most people will ever understand.

Because of these intense physical challenges, I actually have a caregiver for 103 hours a month to help me manage my daily activities. Let me say that again, because people tend to gloss over it: one hundred and three hours a month. That's roughly twenty-five hours a week of dedicated assistance just to handle the basics of daily living. This is not something I'm ashamed of; this is something I want people to understand. Disability is not a moral failing, and needing help is not weakness. The caregiver system exists for exactly this reason, and I am grateful for it even as I wish I didn't need it.

The chronic pain flares are unpredictable and debilitating. Some days I can code for twelve hours straight and feel almost normal. Other days I can barely get out of bed. The unpredictability is almost worse than the pain itself, because it makes planning impossible and forces me to be constantly adaptive. I have learned to work in bursts, to front-load my productivity when I'm feeling good, and to be gentle with myself when my body decides it's not participating today. This rhythm has shaped my entire approach to work, to relationships, to life itself.

I manage my healthcare through Yakima Valley Farm Workers Clinic and my insurance through Coordinated Care Apple Health. Navigating the healthcare system as someone with multiple chronic conditions is practically a full-time job, and I have become an expert in it out of necessity. I know my HIPAA rights inside and out. I understand the insurance appeals process. I can read a medical record with the same analytical precision I apply to source code. When the system fails me — and it frequently does — I document everything, cite the relevant statutes, and advocate for myself with the same intensity I bring to everything else. Being a patient in the American healthcare system requires advocacy skills that most people never develop, and I have developed them to a fine art.

I am also lactose intolerant, which dictates a lot of my food choices. It's a minor thing compared to everything else, but it's part of the daily calculus of living in this body. I still manage to enjoy my Taco Bell and Sonic orders; I've just learned which items won't make me regret my choices. Managing a body with this many constraints requires constant attention, constant adjustment, and a dark sense of humor that helps me get through the worst days.

The Mind I Navigate: Schizophrenia, Bipolar 1, BPD, and CPTSD

My mental health journey is just as complex as my physical one, and I believe in destigmatizing it by being entirely open. I have been officially diagnosed with Schizophrenia, Bipolar 1, Borderline Personality Disorder, and Complex PTSD. That's four major diagnoses, each one significant on its own, all coexisting in the same brain. It is a lot to manage. It requires constant vigilance, constant medication management, and a support system that knows exactly what to do when things go sideways. I am not ashamed of any of these diagnoses. They are facts about my brain, not judgments about my character.

The combination of intense chronic pain and these heavy mental health conditions frequently triggers severe dissociative episodes. These are not simple moments of spacing out. They are periods where I lose connection with reality, with my body, with my sense of self. Time becomes unreliable. Memory fragments. The world feels distant and unreal. Coming out of a dissociative episode is disorienting and exhausting, and sometimes I need hours to fully return to baseline. These episodes are scary, but I have learned to manage them with protocols and systems rather than panic.

I have strict safety protocols in place for dissociation. If I do not come out of an episode after two hours, my automated system automatically messages Darren to check on me. This is not a theoretical safeguard; this is a real, tested, battle-proven protocol that has kept me safe more times than I can count. The system is built on the same monitoring infrastructure I use for my servers, because when your brain is the production environment, uptime monitoring becomes a medical necessity. Darren has the access, the knowledge, and the steady presence needed to help me through the worst episodes, and I trust him completely.

Bipolar 1 means I experience both manic and depressive episodes. The manic episodes can be productive — I have written thousands of lines of code during manic phases — but they can also be dangerous, leading to impulsive decisions and sleep deprivation that feeds back into the psychosis. The depressive episodes are heavy, dark, and draining in ways that words struggle to capture. Borderline Personality Disorder intensifies my emotional responses to relationships, making me feel everything at maximum volume, especially fear of abandonment and interpersonal conflict. Complex PTSD comes from years of accumulated trauma, and it shows up in unexpected ways — triggers I can't predict, reactions I can't control, memories that surface without warning.

I manage all of this with a combination of medication, therapy, support systems, and sheer stubbornness. I refuse to let my diagnoses define me, but I also refuse to pretend they don't exist. They are part of my reality, part of my daily experience, and part of what makes me who I am. Being open about them is my way of fighting the stigma that keeps so many people silent about their struggles. If reading this helps even one person feel less alone in their own mental health journey, then every word was worth writing.

Medications and Healthcare: The Full-Time Job of Staying Alive

I take celecoxib for my physical pain, a medication I get through Cost Plus Drugs because it is literally lifesaving and I cannot afford to deal with insurance gatekeeping for every refill. Celecoxib is a COX-2 inhibitor, a type of NSAID that reduces inflammation without the gastrointestinal side effects that make other NSAIDs impossible for me to take long-term. Managing chronic inflammation is a daily battle, and celecoxib is one of my primary weapons in that fight. Cost Plus Drugs, Mark Cuban's project to make medications affordable by cutting out pharmacy benefit managers, has been an absolute godsend for me.

My automated systems remind me to take my daily multivitamin and all my other medications on schedule. I built these reminders myself because commercial solutions weren't reliable enough for someone whose medication adherence is literally a matter of safety. Missing a dose of psychiatric medication can trigger a cascade of symptoms that takes days or weeks to stabilize. Missing a dose of pain medication means hours of unnecessary suffering. The reminders aren't a convenience; they're part of my medical infrastructure, as essential as the medications themselves.

My healthcare is managed through a complex web of providers, insurance systems, and self-advocacy. Coordinated Care Apple Health is my insurance, and it covers most of what I need, but navigating the system requires constant attention. Prior authorizations, formulary changes, network restrictions, billing errors — these are not occasional annoyances; they are recurring obstacles that I have to clear every single month. I have become my own case manager out of necessity, and I have developed a level of expertise in healthcare navigation that I wish no one ever had to develop.

My complete medical record exists as a 15MB PDF on the servers of Yakima Valley Farm Workers Clinic. That PDF contains everything: every diagnosis, every medication, every lab result, every clinical note. It also contains my deadname alongside my current name, because medical records don't forget. This is one of the reasons I take data security so seriously — a breach of that PDF would expose not just my identity but my complete medical and transition history in combination. I monitor for data breaches obsessively because the stakes are genuinely that high.

I recently had to deliver my identification documents to a doctor's office, and instead of using their insecure web portal, I delivered them in a cryptographically signed, GPG-verified, AES-256 encrypted zip file with a full integrity manifest. My handwritten signature was captured via a browser-based signature pad with embedded X.509 digital certificate verification. I cited the relevant HIPAA statutes, explained exactly which security headers their portal was missing, and provided them with compliant alternatives. I am not a security researcher; I am a patient who knows enough to protect her own data when the system won't do it for her. That's the level of healthcare advocacy I have been forced to develop.

How My Brain Works: Visual Stress, Audiobooks, and Keyboard Feel

Here is something most people don't know about me, and it's important: I have a hard time reading when text is black on white, but I read great with white on black (or light text on a dark background). This is not a preference; this is a neurological reality. The condition is called visual stress, also known as Meares-Irlen syndrome. It is a perceptual processing disorder where high-contrast patterns, particularly black text on white backgrounds, cause visual distortion, eye strain, headaches, and a sensation that the text is moving, blurring, or vibrating on the page. For people with visual stress, reading on a white background is physically exhausting and often painful — the brain is working overtime to process a visual input that it can't properly stabilize.

Visual stress is not a vision problem in the traditional sense; it's a cortical processing issue. The eyes are seeing fine, but the visual cortex is struggling to interpret the high-contrast pattern. About 20% of the population experiences some degree of visual stress, and it's significantly more common in people who are neurodivergent or have conditions like dyslexia, autism, ADHD, or — as in my case — schizophrenia and bipolar disorder. The solution is elegantly simple: reduce the contrast. Dark mode isn't just an aesthetic choice for me; it's an accessibility necessity. I designed this entire book reader with white text on a dark background specifically because that's how my brain works best, and if you're reading this and realizing that dark mode helps you too, you're not alone.

Because of visual stress, I listen to audiobooks far more than I read print books. Audiobooks bypass the visual processing bottleneck entirely and let me absorb information through a channel that doesn't cause physical discomfort. I can listen at 1.5x or 2x speed and retain every detail, whereas reading the same material in print would take me twice as long and leave me with a headache. This isn't laziness; it's adaptive strategy. When your brain processes information differently, you find the paths that work, and audiobooks are one of my most reliable paths.

When I type, something unusual happens: I don't actually take in the information I am typing very well. Instead, I am focused on exactly how it feels whenever I am typing on a specific keyboard. The tactile sensation of the keys, the actuation force, the keycap profile, the switch type, the sound profile — these are what my brain latches onto during typing. The content flows through my fingers to the screen, but my conscious attention is on the physical experience of the keyboard itself. This is part of why I have such strong opinions about keyboards and why I've spent so much time optimizing my input setup. The keyboard isn't just a tool; it's a sensory experience that my brain prioritizes in ways that most people's don't.

This sensory prioritization extends beyond keyboards. I process the world through texture, through sound, through the way things feel rather than just how they look. It's why I can spend hours optimizing a terminal color scheme or tuning my desktop environment's animations. These aren't frivolous details; they're accessibility adjustments that make computing sustainable for a brain that experiences sensory input differently. I have built my entire computing environment around my neurological needs, and the result is a system that works for me in ways that off-the-shelf solutions never could.

Chapter 10

The Birth of AcreetionOS: Two Republicans, One Arch Distro

AcreetionOS was born from a simple question: what if Arch Linux was actually accessible to normal people? Arch is powerful, flexible, and puts the user in control — but it also has a well-deserved reputation for being intimidating, requiring extensive manual configuration, and expecting users to be Linux experts from day one. Darren and I looked at this and thought: what if we could bridge the gap? What if we could take the power and flexibility of Arch and wrap it in a polished, user-friendly experience that didn't sacrifice the control that makes Arch special? What if an Arch distro could be libre, fast, and simple without being dumbed down?

The project is co-led by Darren Clift (Co-Lead Developer, Traditional Republican) and myself, Natalie Cole-Clift Spiva (Co-Lead Developer, 24, Trans Girl, Republican). We are a father-daughter team, and our dynamic is one of the things that makes AcreetionOS unique. Two Republicans from different generations, one traditional and one trans, building software together because we both believe in individual liberty, personal responsibility, and the power of open source. People often ask how a trans girl can be a Republican, and our answer is consistent: identity and politics are separate things. Our focus is on building great software for everyone, regardless of background.

AcreetionOS ships with the Cinnamon desktop environment, X11/XLibre display server support, PipeWire audio, and a curated selection of over 250 packages. It uses the archiso build framework, squashfs compression with xz and x86 BCJ filter optimization, and supports both BIOS and UEFI boot modes. The Calamares installer provides a graphical installation experience, and the distribution comes pre-configured with Firefox, development tools, hardware support firmware, and a suite of system utilities. Every package is tested and verified before it reaches users — no blind bundling, no untrusted sources.

The project has multiple editions: AcreetionOS Standard (Cinnamon on X11), AcreetionOS XL (XLibre variant), AcreetionOS Mate (MATE desktop), AcreetionOS Hyprland (Wayland compositor), AcreetionOS Server (headless, minimal, hardened), AcreetionOS GNOME, and even a 32-bit (i686) experimental build. We also have an AcreetionOS Immutable project (formerly ArttulOS) focused on absolute stability. The ecosystem extends to an app store, a media writer tool, a Flasher Tauri desktop app, a Flatpak repository (FlatFree), and comprehensive documentation.

The website at acreetionos.org serves as the project hub, with downloads hosted on our own ISO server, documentation powered by an AI-enhanced Arch Wiki integration, a newsletter written by AI via OpenRouter and Buttondown, and community spaces on Discord and GitLab. The entire infrastructure — package repositories, ISO hosting, GitLab, mirrors, AI models — runs on servers that we manage ourselves. Repository sovereignty is a core value: we maintain full control over our package repositories, ensuring that every component is curated, tested, and verified before reaching users. We answer to our users, not shareholders, and our roadmap is shaped by community needs, not corporate interests.

The Hardware and Infrastructure: 128GB RAM, Self-Hosting, and Enterprise Gear

My current daily driver rig is an absolute beast. It packs a 1660 Ti GPU, 128GB of RAM, and 48 third-generation Intel CPU threads. That's not a typo. One hundred and twenty-eight gigabytes of memory, forty-eight threads. This machine is the backbone of everything I do: running local AI models, building ISOs, compiling kernels, hosting services, and generally pushing the limits of what a single workstation can accomplish. When people ask me why I need 128GB of RAM, I show them the twenty-something Docker containers, the local LLM inference, the kernel compilation jobs, and the virtual machines all running simultaneously, and they stop asking.

I am absolutely obsessed with enterprise-grade hardware. There is nothing quite like taking a Dell Precision workstation or an HP ProLiant server and optimizing it to run perfectly in a home lab environment. Enterprise gear is built to different standards than consumer hardware — it's designed for 24/7 operation, it has redundant components, it has management interfaces that let you control it remotely, and it can be had for a fraction of its original cost on the secondary market. Building out a home lab with decommissioned enterprise hardware is one of my favorite activities. It's like adopting retired racehorses and giving them a second life where they can run at their own pace.

I self-host almost all of my essential services because I believe in maintaining total control over my own digital infrastructure and personal data. That means running my own private instances of GitLab, Nextcloud, and Mastodon. YAcFS and the Filesystem Revolution

We built YAcFS — Yet Another common File System — because the existing filesystem landscape is broken. ZFS is trapped behind a CDDL licensing wall that makes it impossible to ship as part of the Linux kernel. Every distro that wants ZFS has to jump through hoops with DKMS, out-of-tree modules, and license incompatibility fights. It's 2026 and we're still dealing with this. ZFS is also a memory hog, requires tuning to run well on commodity hardware, and its RAID-Z expansion is still painful. btrfs was supposed to be the answer — it's in the kernel, it's GPL, it has all the features. But after a decade and a half, it still has edge cases that eat your data. The RAID56 code is still considered dangerous, send/receive can produce corrupted streams under load, and performance on spinning rust is genuinely bad. We've lost data to btrfs. We're done trusting it.

Everything else (ext4, XFS) is a journaling filesystem from the 90s. No checksums. No snapshots. No compression. No self-healing. In 2026, storing data without integrity verification is irresponsible. So we wrote YAcFS — the filesystem that should have existed all along. YAcFS is GPLv3 (no licensing bullshit, ships in every kernel, every distro), fast (writeback cache, kernel page cache, block-level content dedup, xxhash3 checksums, ZSTD compression tuned for speed), simple (one binary, one pool directory, mount it and go), safe (checksums on every read, content-addressable blocks, COW snapshots), and pooled (union multiple directories into one filesystem pool). We built it because we were tired of choosing between "works with Linux" and "has basic data integrity features." YAcFS gives you both — and outperforms both ZFS and btrfs on real-world workloads.

The YAcFS ecosystem spans multiple repositories: yacfs-kmod (the kernel module), yacfs-tools (userspace utilities), yacfs-metrics (performance monitoring), and yacfs-replicate (data replication). The documentation lives on the Sprungles.org site under docs/yacfs/ and covers architecture, usage, performance benchmarks, and API documentation. YAcFS represents everything I believe about software: if something is broken, don't complain about it. Build the thing that fixes it. Make it libre. Make it fast. Make it simple. Ship it. Let the world decide if it's better. I know it is.

I am defiant by nature, and I am proud by choice. Every single day that I wake up and choose to be authentically Natalie is a victory.

Chapter 24

Daily Life and Routines: Coffee Pods, Taco Bell, 103 Hours of Care

My daily life is a carefully orchestrated balancing act between managing my health, maintaining my infrastructure, and pushing forward on my projects. A typical day starts with coffee — always coffee, non-negotiable — followed by checking my monitoring dashboards for any alerts. I review my medication reminders, check in on my self-hosted services (GitLab, Nextcloud, Mastodon, ISO server, package repository), and scan for any deadname mentions or data breach alerts. This morning routine takes about an hour, and it's the foundation that everything else is built on.

My caregiver is with me for about 25 hours a week (of the 103 monthly hours I'm approved for), helping with the physical tasks that my chronic pain makes difficult. This isn't a luxury; it's a medical necessity. The caregiver helps with mobility, household tasks, meal preparation, and anything else that my body decides it can't handle on a given day. Having this support is the difference between being able to focus on my work and being completely consumed by the logistics of existing in a body with autoimmune disease.

Food is a constant calculus. Lactose intolerant means I have to be careful with dairy. Chronic pain means I often don't have the energy to cook elaborate meals. Taco Bell, Sonic, and Little Caesars fill the gap when I'm deep in a coding session and can't break focus. It's not glamorous, but it's sustainable, and sustainability is what matters. I keep a supply of coffee pods stocked at all times because the caffeine is essential for managing the fatigue that comes with my conditions and medications.

My insurance is through Coordinated Care Apple Health, a Washington State Medicaid plan. My healthcare provider is Yakima Valley Farm Workers Clinic. Managing appointments, prior authorizations, prescriptions, and referrals across these systems requires constant attention and advocacy. I've become expert at navigating the bureaucracy, knowing exactly which forms need to be filed, which statutes apply, and how to escalate when things go wrong. The American healthcare system is not designed for people with complex, overlapping conditions, and surviving it requires skills that no one should have to develop.

Despite all of this, I find genuine joy in my daily routines. The morning coffee ritual. The satisfaction of checking my monitoring dashboards and seeing green across the board. The flow state of a good coding session. The comfort of delivery food when I'm too tired to cook. The moments of connection with Bre, even from two thousand miles away. These small pleasures are not distractions from my struggles; they are the reasons I keep fighting. A life worth living is built from these moments, and I collect them deliberately.

Chapter 25

The Future: SCC, Hands-Free Computing, and Building a Better World

This fall, I start classes at Spokane Community College. It's a new chapter in a life that has already had more chapters than most people my age. I'm approaching SCC with the same intensity I bring to everything: I'm going to learn everything they can teach me, and then I'm going to go beyond it. The degree is not the goal; the knowledge is. Every class, every assignment, every interaction is an opportunity to become better at building the things I want to build, and I intend to extract maximum value from every single one of those opportunities.

My hands-free computing interface project is one of the most important things I'm working on right now. By leveraging tools like OpenGo and DeepSeek, I'm building ways to minimize keyboard usage and make computing more accessible and efficient. This isn't just about my own accessibility needs; it's about building infrastructure that could help millions of people with motor disabilities, chronic pain, or conditions that make traditional computing interfaces difficult or impossible. The goal is a computing experience where you can navigate, code, and create without ever touching a keyboard — all driven by AI that runs locally, privately, and under your control.

AcreetionOS continues to evolve. The immutable variant (formerly ArttulOS) is a side project focused on absolute stability and a future where computing exists in the "greys," providing a seamless experience without the burden of constant choice. The FlatFree Flatpak repository is growing. YAcFS is getting faster and more stable with every commit. The package repository keeps expanding with new packages and updates. The documentation keeps getting better. The community keeps growing. AcreetionOS is not a snapshot; it's a trajectory, and the trajectory is pointed firmly upward.

My long-term vision is a world where everyone owns their stack. Where software serves people, not corporations. Where the tools we use every day are transparent, auditable, and under our control. Where being trans, being disabled, being neurodivergent — none of it limits your access to technology that works for you. I'm not just building an OS; I'm building proof that another way is possible. Every libre tool I create, every proprietary service I replace with a self-hosted alternative, every accessibility feature I build into my software — all of it is evidence that the future doesn't have to belong to the corporations. It can belong to us.
